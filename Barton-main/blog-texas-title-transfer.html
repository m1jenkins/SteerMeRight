Texas Buyer's Legal Guide

How to Transfer a Car Title in Texas (Step-by-Step)

By Mason • Legal & Process

Whether you bought from a private seller, received a vehicle as a gift, or inherited a car, transferring the title in Texas has specific requirements that must be completed within 30 days. Miss that window and you'll face escalating penalties, potential registration holds, and unnecessary stress at the county tax office. This guide covers every scenario, every form, and every fee so you can walk in prepared and walk out with a clean title.

Title transfer in Texas is administered at the county level through your local county tax assessor-collector's office, not the Texas DMV directly, which is a common point of confusion. Each of the state's 254 counties processes title work, and while the forms and fees are standardized statewide, wait times and office procedures can vary significantly. Plan accordingly, bring every document listed below, and you'll avoid the dreaded second trip.

What You Need Before Going to the County Tax Office

Before you set foot in the county tax assessor-collector's office, assemble every required document. Missing even one will result in a wasted trip. Here's the complete checklist:

  • Signed title, The seller must sign the front of the title in the designated area. The buyer completes the buyer section on the back, including the purchase price, date of sale, and odometer reading.
  • Completed Application for Texas Title and/or Registration (Form 130-U), Available at the county office or downloadable from the TxDMV website. Both buyer and seller sections must be filled out.
  • Valid photo ID, Texas driver's license or state-issued identification card for all applicants listed on the title.
  • Proof of liability insurance, Texas requires minimum liability coverage. Bring your insurance card or a digital copy showing the VIN of the vehicle being titled.
  • Vehicle inspection report, Texas requires a passing vehicle inspection within 90 days of registration for most vehicles. Some newer vehicles and certain categories are exempt.
  • Payment for fees and taxes, Be prepared to pay the title application fee, registration fee, and the 6.25% motor vehicle sales tax. Most county offices accept checks, cash, and cards, though some charge a convenience fee for card payments.

Step-by-Step: Private Party Purchase

Private party transactions require the most buyer diligence because there is no dealership handling the paperwork on your behalf. Here is the exact sequence:

Step 1: The seller signs the title on the front in the "Seller's Signature" area and prints their name. They must also complete the odometer disclosure section accurately, federal law requires this for vehicles less than 20 years old.

Step 2: The buyer completes the back of the title, including their full legal name, address, purchase price, and date of sale. Both parties should also complete Form 130-U together, as it requires information from both sides of the transaction.

Step 3: The buyer takes the signed title, completed Form 130-U, valid ID, proof of insurance, and inspection report to their county tax assessor-collector's office within 30 days of the purchase date.

Step 4: At the county office, you'll pay the 6.25% motor vehicle sales tax. Critically, this tax is assessed on the Standard Presumptive Value (SPV) or the purchase price, whichever is higher. You'll also pay the title application fee and registration fees. The county office will issue a registration sticker and temporary permit. Your new title will arrive by mail in approximately four to six weeks.

Step-by-Step: Dealership Purchase

When you buy from a licensed Texas dealer, the title and registration process is significantly simpler because the dealership handles most of the paperwork under their dealer license. The dealer collects the sales tax, submits the title application to the county, and issues you temporary buyer's tags that are valid for up to 60 days while your metal plates are being produced.

However, "the dealer handles it" is not a guarantee that everything was filed correctly. Verify that you received a copy of your completed Form 130-U, confirm the sales tax amount matches the contract, and make a calendar note to follow up if your metal plates and registration sticker haven't arrived within 60 days. If the dealer used temporary tags and you still haven't received permanent plates after 60 days, contact the county tax office directly, the dealer may have failed to submit the paperwork. Understanding Standard Presumptive Value (SPV)

Standard Presumptive Value is the mechanism Texas uses to prevent tax avoidance on private party vehicle sales. The state maintains a database of wholesale vehicle values, and when you transfer a title from a private party sale, the county tax office looks up the vehicle's SPV based on its year, make, model, mileage, and condition category.

If the SPV is higher than the price you actually paid, you will owe the 6.25% tax on the SPV, not on your lower purchase price. This surprises many buyers who assume they'll only be taxed on the amount they negotiated. The only way to override the SPV is to obtain a certified appraisal from a licensed Texas motor vehicle dealer or a licensed insurance adjuster. The appraisal must be on the appraiser's letterhead, include the vehicle's VIN, and state the fair market value. If the certified appraisal supports a lower value, the county office will use it as the tax basis instead. You can check a vehicle's SPV before purchasing at the TxDMV website to avoid surprises at the tax office.

Common Title Transfer Problems

Even straightforward transactions can hit roadblocks. These are the issues we see most frequently:

  • Lienholder not released: If the seller had a loan on the vehicle, the lienholder must release their interest before the title can transfer. If the title still shows a lien, the buyer cannot complete the transfer until the seller obtains a lien release letter from their bank or finance company.
  • Mismatched VIN: Any discrepancy between the VIN on the title and the VIN on the vehicle itself will halt the transfer immediately. This usually indicates a clerical error, but it can also signal fraud. A VIN inspection by law enforcement may be required to resolve the issue.
  • Missing signatures: Both the seller and the buyer must sign in the correct locations on the title. A missing signature, a signature in the wrong field, or use of white-out on a title will result in rejection.
  • Out-of-state titles: Vehicles titled in other states can be transferred to Texas, but the process may require additional documentation, including the out-of-state title, a VIN inspection, and potentially a weight certificate for commercial vehicles.
  • Title jumping: This occurs when a buyer resells a vehicle without ever titling it in their own name, essentially acting as an unlicensed dealer. Title jumping is illegal in Texas and can result in fines, criminal charges, and an untransferable title for the end buyer.
  • Bonded titles: When the original title has been lost and cannot be replaced through normal channels, Texas allows the applicant to purchase a surety bond (typically 1.5 times the vehicle's value) and obtain a bonded title. The bond remains active for five years to protect against ownership claims.

Fees Breakdown

Texas title transfer fees are standardized, but the total cost depends on your specific situation. Here is what to expect:

  • Title application fee: $28 to $33, depending on the county.
  • Motor vehicle sales tax: 6.25% of the purchase price or SPV (whichever is higher) for private party sales. Dealership sales tax is based on the contract price.
  • Registration fee: Varies by vehicle weight and type, but typically ranges from $50.75 to $54.75 for standard passenger vehicles.
  • Vehicle inspection fee: $7.00 for a standard two-year inspection at a certified inspection station (paid separately before visiting the county office).
  • Late transfer penalty: If you transfer the title after the 30-day window, penalties start at $25 and can increase based on how late the application is filed. Don't procrastinate.

In total, a typical private party title transfer on a $15,000 vehicle will cost approximately $1,025 to $1,050 when you include the 6.25% sales tax, title fee, and registration. Budget for this amount in addition to the vehicle's purchase price.

Gift Transfers & Family Transfers

Texas offers a significant tax advantage for vehicles transferred between eligible family members. Instead of paying the standard 6.25% motor vehicle sales tax, qualifying gift transfers are subject to a flat $10 gift tax, regardless of the vehicle's value. For a $30,000 vehicle, that's a savings of $1,865 compared to the standard tax rate.

To qualify, both the donor and the recipient must complete Form 14-317 (Affidavit of Motor Vehicle Gift Transfer). Eligible family members include spouses, parents, children (including stepchildren), siblings, grandparents, and grandchildren. The vehicle must be gifted, not sold for any amount, even a nominal one. If money changes hands, the county office will assess standard sales tax. The completed Form 14-317 is submitted alongside the signed title, Form 130-U, proof of insurance, and valid ID at the county tax office.

"The most common title transfer mistake is showing up at the county office without a lien release. If the seller had a loan, you need proof it's been paid off, or you will be turned away."

Transferring a car title in Texas is procedurally straightforward, but the details matter enormously. A single missing document, an unsigned field, or a misunderstanding about SPV can turn a 30-minute errand into weeks of back-and-forth.
